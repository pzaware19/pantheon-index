The Pantheon: Measuring Greatness and Grace Across Three Sports

Can you measure the sublime? This puts Sachin Tendulkar, Lionel Messi and Roger Federer on a single scale, half of it about dominance and half about beauty.

Piyush Zaware June 2026 Constructed metric · curated data

The premise

Ask a cricket fan to name the most elegant batsman and you'll hear Tendulkar. In football the word "genius" tends to arrive with Messi. In tennis, grace means Federer. They aren't just the best in their sports. They're the ones whose greatness happened to look beautiful. So here's the question this piece chases. Three athletes, three sports that have nothing to do with each other, one shared quality. Can you actually put a number on it?

Sort of. It works if you're careful about what "comparable" even means, and most of what follows is about being careful.

The claim, up front

All three finish first in their own sport on a composite dominance score. And when you plot the two axes together, they trace an efficient frontier: no merely-great peer is both more dominant and more elegant than they are. Messi and Tendulkar push the dominance edge. Federer sets the elegance ceiling. Between them they fence off the corner, the way a Pareto frontier fences off everything that's good but not great.

The comparison problem

You can't line up a Test batting average (Tendulkar around 54), goals per ninety minutes (Messi north of 1.0) and a career match-win rate (Federer about 82%). Different units, different scales, different games. Put those raw numbers in a table, rank them, and you've measured nothing.

The fix comes from the biologist Stephen Jay Gould, who used it to compare baseball hitters from different eras. Stop comparing the outputs. Compare where a player sits inside their own distribution. Not "how many runs" but "how many standard deviations clear of your peers were you." A z-score carries no units, so being 1.5σ above the field means the same thing in cricket, football and tennis. That one move is what makes the rest of this possible.

The data

The pipeline reads two small tables, both put together by hand.

  • players.csv holds each star plus about 8 peers from their own era and sport (Lara, Ponting, Kallis, Sangakkara; Ronaldo, Lewandowski, Mbappé; Nadal, Djokovic, Sampras), each scored on five pillars.
  • commentary_corpus.csv is a set of press and commentary lines about each star. It feeds the elegance axis and nothing else.

The numbers are honest approximations, picked to get the magnitudes right rather than scraped to four decimals. What matters here is the method. Everything reads from those CSVs, so a bigger dataset slots straight in and every figure below recomputes itself.

Methods

The five pillars of dominance

Every player gets scored on five pillars, all of them "higher is better". Each pillar becomes a z-score against that sport's peers, and we average the five.

  • Peak. How high the best stretch got (a peak-era rate stat).
  • Longevity. How many elite years they sustained.
  • Consistency. The share of seasons spent at an elite level.
  • Big-Stage. Knockouts, World Cups, major finals.
  • Volume. Total output stacked up against peers.

Since the z-score has no units, the average (call it Standardized Dominance) reads the same across all three sports.

The elegance lexicon

The second axis is the fun one. Instead of inventing an "elegance stat", it measures how people write about these players. Every commentary line gets checked against a list of grace-and-beauty words (graceful, effortless, sublime, artistry, poetry, silk, balletic, mesmerising), and the score is how densely that language shows up per hundred words, rescaled to 100. It's simple, anyone can reproduce it, and it lands about where fans already feel it should.

Results: Standardized Dominance

Here's each star's composite z-score against their own sport's field. All three come out on top, which is worth pausing on. Three completely different peer groups, three winners, and they're our three.

The composite hides how they got there, so the radars break it back out, each pillar as a percentile within the sport. Greatness comes in different shapes. Tendulkar's strength is Volume and Longevity. Messi's is a huge Peak. Federer's is Consistency and Longevity, held up across two decades.

Measuring the sublime

Here the score comes from the words, not the play. The more often writers reach for beauty-words about a player, the higher they land. And which words they reach for says a lot about what kind of beautiful each one was.

Federer pins the top of the scale. That isn't the lexicon flattering him. Call it the David Foster Wallace effect: no athlete in any sport has drawn as much openly aesthetic writing ("Federer as religious experience"). Tendulkar's language circles timing and wristwork. Messi's circles the dribble and the dance. All three score high, which is exactly what you'd want.

A caveat about the corpus

The corpus is curated, not scraped. A version you could really defend would run this same lexicon over thousands of match reports, and the scorer is built to take one. For now, read Figure 3 as a proof of concept rather than a final number.

The Greatness × Elegance plane

Put both axes on one chart and the argument shows up. Each athlete is a dot. Further right means more dominant over peers, further up means more elegant in the writing. Peers sit along the dominance axis for context. Our three trace the dashed frontier: Federer holds the top, Messi and Tendulkar hold the right, and nobody sits above and to the right of the line. That frontier, rather than any single corner, is the real shape of what they have in common.

What we find

Squash the plane into one 50/50 blend and you get an order, though the blend is only one choice among many.

Roughly: Messi shades the dominance axis on the strength of the biggest single peak of the three. Tendulkar sits just behind on a longevity-and-volume case. Federer runs away with elegance so decisively that he stays right in the mix even though his rivals, Nadal and Djokovic, are about as tough a peer group as sport has ever produced. But the ranking isn't the point. The point is that three athletes from three sports end up in the same small patch of a chart that tracks both how far clear of everyone they were and how good it looked.

Why this is harder to answer than it looks

To take the question seriously, here's what this thing genuinely can't do.

  • Peer fields aren't equally deep. Federer might just have the toughest peer group in the study, which drags his z-score down next to someone who ruled a weaker field. The z-score adjusts for era inside a sport, not for how loaded that era was.
  • The weights are a judgement call. Averaging the five pillars equally is reasonable but arbitrary. Tilt it toward volume or peak and the order shuffles.
  • The elegance axis measures writing, not playing. It picks up how beautifully people described a player, which tracks beauty but also fame, language, and whether a good writer happened to be watching.
  • Three points can't hold up a serious blend. The 50/50 "Pantheon score" is there to present, not to prove. The plane is the real finding. The leaderboard is decoration.
The honest bottom line

This is a made-up metric, not a law of nature. What it's good for is turning a gut feeling, that these three share something, into something explicit you can reproduce and argue with. Change the data, move the weights, grow the corpus, and watch it shift. A good index should let you do exactly that.
